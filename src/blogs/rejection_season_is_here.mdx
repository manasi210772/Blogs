# Winter's over, it's Rejection season

It had to happen, its been a long time coming. A rejection, finally at last. I have got to get used to these. Contrarily, I thought I would get a job rejection first.
But no its from a research journal.

## Context

I am pursuing my Bachelors in Engineering right now and as part of the coursework, we have a major project marking the culmination of our degrees. The magnum
opus, the grand finale of this 4 year long journey. By we, I mean a group of 3 students. We've been working on this project for a whole semester now. It is multi-class
oil spill segmentation. Under the "Major Project" course, well the course outcome is having a paper published in either a conference or a journal. So, we had to
write our very first research paper and this is how it went.

## Writing the paper

We had never written a research paper before this. For our mini projects, which are semester long projects that we've had to do as part of the coursework in second
and third year, we did make reports. These reports are not comparable to an actual research paper, but it still is the closest thing to it.
However, all these reports were more of less AI generated,
thats not something we can do with our paper. Journals and conferences have strict plagiarism and AI generated content detection checks. Initially to get the crux
of the material we did use AI and then paraphrased it manually and later fixed up the grammar as well.

Firstly, we were making a journal agnostic draft focusing on the content. A few followups with our guide, fixing things here and there. We reached a point where we
could start submitting. Our guide suggested [MethodsX by Science Direct](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/methodsx). Here, we'd be publishing a methods article
where we'd describe our method in detail. After, creating our submission in accordance with their format, we went to our guide. She said our paper still didn't look
"publish-worthy". There was a lot of text and it was too theoretical.

This started the diagrams saga. Diagrams are pain. From coming up with an idea, to visualizing
the idea, laying out the idea, the colours, the formatting, there's a 100 things to think about. Our guide did suggest [Napkin](https://www.napkin.ai/) for getting quick prototypes for diagrams, and [draw.io](https://www.drawio.com/) for making the actual diagrams. We eventually ended up adding a lot of diagrams, which made the paper look a bit, well not dead.
All this time, I was iterating and <a href="/rajnikam/blogs/improving_oil">improving the project as well</a>, which made the whole ordeal worse. Also, tables and figures have different ways of being addressed. Small things like these really add up and slow you down:

For references, we had to use [Mendeley](https://www.mendeley.com/). I initially thought references were purely visual and we just had to follow the format of the
journal that we're submitting to. Well I was correct but keeping track of 20-30 references and their numbering can get messy. Also, all this while we were juggling
our files between MS Office, Google Docs, Libreoffice, that was another mess. For Mendeley, MS Office was the only option so with no git-like version controle or well google docs in place, people working on older versions. Fixed issues
getting unfixed, doing the same work again, things flying under the radar. We ran face-first into every obstacle that could possibly exist.

But with the new diagrams,
better results and also reduced text, our guide was happy with our work. One last check using [Turnitin](https://www.turnitin.com/) she checked our AI and plagiarism
scores. We were warned that menial things like equations do get flagged and if the overall plagiarism is over 10% we might have to change notations in the equations,
because well thats the only thing interchangeable per se. The results were pretty shocking, well for now atleast in a good way. 0% for AI and 2% for plagiarism, we
were ecstatic, like blown away. We were so skeptical of ourselves, we had to confirm the <b>\"\*"</b> actually meant 0%. With that we were geared up to publish.

## Submitting the paper

Our guide had already made successful submissions to this journal. We did catch a glimpse of previous submissions, there were 4 and 3 of them were rejected (<em>Not so subtle foreshadowing</em>). We were submitting through her account, and while following the procedure she did remind us of what the journal expected, it was this exact text:

> A method article is a short, templated article, describing methodological advances from all subject areas. The article can also describe customizations to previously established methods. Articles should also include evidence of the efficiency of the method.

I had done majority of the work on this project, while the other members nodded along and reaffirmed that this lines up with what we had done, I was pretty unsure.
I didn't vocalize my concern with her, but I did tell my team members about it afterwards. The submission was done, got an ORCID account up, got it linked up, confirmed our affiliation with the paper. And before even waiting anxiously for what was to come, our submission was already rejected.

## What went wrong?

Well we got an auto-reject template message and it went as follows:

> The manuscript you submitted does not fit our scope. Please be advised that MethodsX is a methods journal, and not a conventional research journal. At MethodsX we publish the following article types:
> • Method articles describe new methods in all research areas.<br />
> • Protocol articles focus on the life and health science areas. <br />
> • Review articles review and discuss methodological information.<br />
> • Letters to the Editor are commentaries on methods/protocols previously published.<br />

> Your submission appears to be a full-length research article, which are not published by MethodsX. Clear indicators of this are that results and/or conclusions are presented in the manuscript, which are not part of Method or Protocol articles. Method and Protocol articles are limited to the description of a method or protocol, strictly following the templates we offer for these article types"

One thing that we did extra was adding a "Conclusion" heading which wasn't present in the original format. Although, that on its own doesn't seem enough for
a rejection, it might have been a catalyst. Instead of focusing on the method, we focused on results which the journal had explicitly mentioned it doesn't care that
much about. We should've read papers that were already accepted. We focused more on the results, on the comparisons and how our results are better instead of working on something new architecturally or introducing a smart trick. That is where we messed up big time. Just aligning our work with their agenda. Although, you can also
argue that the choice of the journal should've also been evaluated better.

## What now?

Well its only one rejection. Kind of glad that it happened quickly and we didn't have to waste time waiting for it. We'll now search for journals and conferences
that are aligned with the work that we've done. Rejections are pretty normal. It's definitely not ideal but hey we're not hurting (yet). I'll probably iterate more, find out new things that I can tweak in the implementation, introduce a smart little trick, have something unique that we're offering. I don't know about applying to
MethodsX again. Although, author identites are kept anonymous for reviewers and editors in the early part atleast (is what I am aware of, clearly don't know what happens if the work is accepted). We know other groups who've got their work published and for them too, it wasn't first try, happy go lucky. They did have their fair
share of rejections as well (well it was only 2). Still, not the end of the world, got to keep moving.
